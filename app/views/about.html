About 'Shut Up, Seattle'

This site is the product of a month-long Ada Developers Academy capstone project by Kat Patke and Kristina Hjertberg.

Seattle can be a pretty noisy place, especially given how quickly it's developing and growing. That can be tricky to navigate if you'd rather not live across the street from an active demolition site (or maybe that's your thing, we don't know your life). Our site will hopefully offer up a clearer picture about what you're getting yourself into before you sign a lease or plan a would-be peaceful stroll through South Lake Union, particularly if you're new to the city.

The noise datapoints available to us (as seen on the main page) are:

  • Fire stations
  • Police stations
  • Emergency rooms/hospitals
  • Active construction sites (where a permit has been issued)
  • Active demolition sites (where a permit has been issued)
  • Schools
  • Colleges
  • Recent noise complaints called into 911
  • Dumps
  • Stadiums
  • Transit (transit centers and bus stops)
  • Bars
  • Airports/Heliports
  • Freeways (including on/off ramps in many cases)

The Noise Rating

Searching a Seattle address or dropping a pin on the map will tell you the estimated noise score for that location. This score is determined by:

  • Finding any noise data points that are within range (approximately one block on all sides)
  • Calculating approximately how loud (in dB) those noises would be at your location using a formula called inverse square law
  • Adding them together and assigning a score, A-F, based on where the total dB falls in our algorithm's range

The location rating also displays a list of which in-range noises were detected.

Unfortunately, our site cannot detect the presence of upstairs neighbors who play the saxophone at 4 in the morning. You're on your own there.
